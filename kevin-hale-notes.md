# Kevin Hale — How to Evaluate Startup Ideas (Part 2)

## Core thesis
Your goal is clarity. A good investor will do the rest — they'll hear what you say and imagine the path to a billion-dollar company. You don't need to sell them. You need to be understood.

## The three questions investors evaluate
1. Do I understand it?
2. Am I excited by it?
3. Do I like the team?

That's it. Everything else is decoration.

## Be clear

A clear idea is the foundation for growth. The best companies grow by word of mouth. Word of mouth only works if people can remember what you do and repeat it at a dinner table.

"Marketing and advertising is a tax companies pay because they didn't make something remarkable."

### Four things that kill clarity

1. **Ambiguity** — can be interpreted two ways, requires a follow-up question
2. **Complexity** — multiple ideas braided together. A simple idea is one idea that doesn't fold.
3. **Mystery** — jargon, vague pronouns ("it", "this"), things you suggest but don't say explicitly
4. **Ignorable language** — marketing speak, MBA speak, buzzwords. Investors have heard these words so many times they literally stop hearing them. Parts of your pitch become invisible.

### What to do instead

- Be conversational. "A great pitch is one where you can tell it to your mom and she gets it."
- No jargon
- No preamble — go straight to what you do
- Be reproducible — when I hear your idea, can I picture it in my head? Can I see what I'd have to build?

## The three nouns

Every startup description needs three nouns:
1. What are you making?
2. What is the problem?
3. Who is the customer?

If I don't have these three nouns, I can't start evaluating.

## Good examples vs bad

**Bad:** "We are going to transform the relationship between individuals and information." — All abstract nouns. Zero informational value. Can't reproduce it.

**Bad:** Long preamble about the story of the company, the problem history, then finally what you do. "I have to read another thousand applications."

**Good:** "Airbnb is the first online marketplace that lets travelers book rooms with locals instead of hotels." — Concise. Descriptive. I know what they're building.

**Good:** "Dropbox synchronizes files across your computers." — No pretense. No defense. No padding.

**Good:** "Lumini is building X-ray vision for soldiers and first responders." — Creates a foundation for curiosity. Now I ask: how? right team? traction?

## Be concise

Concision signals two things to investors:
1. You've thought deeply about your idea (because you can compress it)
2. You're efficient — with words, thoughts, and actions

"If you're efficient with your words, you're probably efficient with your thoughts. And that means you're probably efficient with your actions."

### How to compress a description

Start with the nouns. Strip everything else. Then add back only what makes the investor excited.

**Before:** "Our company will make low-cost and low power consumption medical devices based on artificial intelligence and IoT suitable for sub-Saharan communities."

**Process:**
- Extract nouns: company, medical devices, sub-Saharan communities
- The verb is just "making"
- "AI and IoT" = buzzwords, not the defining characteristic
- "Low cost" = interesting (affordable for developing countries)
- "Low power" = maybe, need more context

**After:** "Affordable medical devices for sub-Saharan Africa."

### Common bad descriptions
- "An online e-commerce store" — what's the customer? what's different?
- "IT security services" — too generic
- "A meritocratic decision support system" — for what? for whom?
- "Learn and share knowledge about data science and AI" — sounds like a mission, not a product

## X for Y format

Works when:
- X is a household name (billion-dollar company everyone knows)
- Everyone agrees X is successful
- Y clearly wants what X does
- Y is a huge market

Doesn't work: "Buffer for Snapchat" — Buffer is smaller than Snapchat.

## Don't play defense

Founders often give up clarity to make themselves look bigger or protect against objections. They add padding, qualifiers, caveats. All that padding pushes the investor further away from you.

"No pretense. They aren't playing defense." — on why Dropbox's description works.

"Your business is complex. I know that. There's no investor that thinks any business is simple and easy. You shouldn't be worried I'm going to think something small of it."

## Don't start with technology

If your description leads with "AI-powered" or "machine learning" or "IoT", Hale sees that as a red flag — it suggests you started with a solution, not a problem. Technology should be a secret weapon, not the headline.

"Those are the buzzwords. Those are the solutions. And it makes me worried that you haven't started with a problem."

## The reproducibility test

Can I picture your company in my head after hearing one sentence? Can I see what I'd have to build? If 10 people heard your description and built 10 companies, would they build roughly the same thing? If not, your description is too vague.

## Concision = competence signal

Being concise isn't just about saving time. It tells investors:
- You've thought deeply about your idea (you can compress it)
- You're efficient with words, so you're probably efficient with actions
- You understand what's most important about your business

"I'll read a thousand applications every batch. Let's make our intimate time really pleasurable. Let's make the time I spend on your idea very efficient."

## The compression process

1. Extract the nouns (what, customer, problem)
2. The verb is usually just "making" or "building"
3. Add back ONLY what makes the investor excited
4. Everything else is dead weight

## Key takeaway

Lead with WHAT, not WHY or HOW. Those get in the way of clarity.

Your one-liner should create a foundation for curiosity — once the investor understands what you do, they'll start asking the right questions themselves. That's your goal. Not answering every question, but getting them to want to ask.
